A Data-Driven Overview of Complaints, Air Pollution,

and Vehicle Numbers in Malaysia

The visualization shows that Selangor and Johor lead in both vehicle registration and environmental complaints in 2019. This correlation highlights the likely environmental pressure on highly urbanized states, where dense populations and high vehicle usage contribute significantly to environmental issues.

The chart above demonstrates that Selangor, Johor, and Pulau Pinang record the highest volumes of vehicle-related complaints, highlighting considerable environmental and urban management challenges in these highly developed states. Conversely, regions like Perlis and Terengganu exhibit notably lower complaint frequencies, which may reflect stronger environmental compliance measures, fewer industrial emissions, or reduced traffic congestion levels compared to their more urbanized counterparts.

The bump chart below portrays the gradual transformation of vehicle-type rankings across Malaysia from 2012-2019. It underscores the sustained rivalry between motorcycles and motorcars for the premier positions, indicating evolving mobility preferences. By contrast, commercial vehicles, taxis, and other transport categories exhibit remarkable stability, remaining in lower tiers with modest variation throughout the observed period. These patterns may inform strategic transportation planning and targeted initiatives in urbanised regions.

This visualization reveals a varied, non-linear relationship between vehicle density and public complaints across Malaysian states. Most cluster at lower rates, while outliers stand out: Negeri Sembilan records the highest complaints despite moderate vehicle density, and W.P. Kuala Lumpur shows extreme vehicle concentration. High complaint levels in Selangor and Penang suggest other contributing factors beyond vehicle numbers.

This multi-year analysis reveals Carbon Monoxide (CO) as Malaysia's predominant air pollutant. Its concentration vastly surpasses that of Ozone (O3), Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2), and Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), which all remain clustered at near-zero levels throughout the period. The data highlights a dramatic spike in CO around September 2019, suggesting a significant seasonal pollution event, likely haze. Apart from this anomaly, CO levels consistently fluctuate within a high range, underscoring its primary role.

This visualization clearly compares the spread of vehicles, area, and population across all Malaysian states using small multiples. Each colorful donut chart shows a unique share pattern, revealing how each state's share differs greatly across the three key measures.
